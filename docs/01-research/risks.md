# Risks

## Product risks

### 1. Scope collapse

- The concept currently spans club operations, community coordination, competition management, and analytics.
- Trying to solve all of them at once would likely produce a bloated product with weak adoption.

### 2. Wrong wedge

- If the first workflow chosen is not painful enough, the product may be interesting in demos but weak in buying urgency.

### 3. Happy-path design

- Real club operations are full of exceptions.
- A product that handles only standard bookings and ideal score entry will break under daily use.

## Market risks

### 4. Crowded category

- The market already includes incumbents and padel-specific suites.
- Broad feature parity is not a viable strategy.

### 5. Habit competition

- WhatsApp, spreadsheets, and existing booking systems are operationally weak but behaviorally strong.
- “Good enough” workflows may block adoption.

### 6. Limited willingness to switch

- Clubs may dislike their current tools and still refuse migration because of data cleanup, training, and operational disruption.

## Business risks

### 7. Weak economic buyer definition

- If the payer is unclear, product strategy and sales motion will drift.

### 8. Monetization mismatch

- Clubs may expect heavy customization while only supporting SMB pricing.
- Community-led buyers may love the idea but resist paying consistently.

### 9. Services trap

- If each club requires deep workflow tailoring, the product may become a consulting-heavy business instead of scalable software.

## Data and trust risks

### 10. Ranking credibility failure

- Opaque ranking logic, weak result verification, or manipulation will destroy trust quickly.

### 11. Low-quality operational data

- Duplicated players, stale availability, and inconsistent result reporting can undermine core workflows.

### 12. Identity complexity

- One person may hold multiple roles across multiple contexts.
- Weak identity and permission design will create admin workarounds and security issues.

## Delivery risks

### 13. Integration dependency

- If the best route is an overlay, the product may depend on APIs and policies owned by other platforms.

### 14. Adoption sequencing risk

- Some features require network density or historical data before they become valuable.
- Bootstrapping that value may be difficult in early rollout.

### 15. Geographic mismatch

- The product may fit one club culture or country workflow and fail elsewhere.

## Strategic risk summary

The biggest risk is not technical. It is building an ambitious “padel operating system” before proving which workflow is painful enough, repeated enough, and commercial enough to justify a new product.
