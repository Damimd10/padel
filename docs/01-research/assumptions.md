# Assumptions

## Product assumptions

- The addressed problems belong in one product rather than in separate tools.
- Reservation management and competition management should coexist in the same initial scope.
- Players care enough about rankings and statistics to keep data current.
- Self-assigned schedules are a real repeated pain point, not an edge case.
- Structured communication will be adopted instead of users defaulting back to WhatsApp.

## User assumptions

- Club operators are willing to change existing workflows if the value is clear.
- Front-desk staff will accept a more structured process during busy periods.
- Players will tolerate profile setup, result confirmation, and status management if it improves access to good matches.
- Tournament organizers want software depth rather than lightweight ad hoc tools.

## Market assumptions

- Existing racquet-sport platforms still leave meaningful padel-specific gaps.
- There is room for a specialized product despite strong incumbents and fast-moving padel-specific vendors.
- The target market is large enough to sustain a focused SaaS product.
- The first target geography has enough club density and digital adoption to support repeatable sales.

## Business assumptions

- The first payer will be a club, organizer, or academy rather than the player.
- The product can prove ROI through time savings, better occupancy, lower no-shows, or higher participation.
- Clubs will pay for operational improvement even if they already pay for another platform.
- Integration or migration costs will not erase commercial value.

## Data and trust assumptions

- Match results can be captured and verified with enough trust to sustain rankings.
- Users will accept the logic behind level, ladder, or rating changes.
- Player identity can be managed without constant duplication and cleanup.
- Availability data can stay fresh enough to support self-service coordination.

## Delivery assumptions

- Discovery interviews will reveal one dominant wedge rather than evenly distributed mild pain.
- Workflow variation across clubs is manageable enough for a reusable product.
- The problem can be solved with a web application and supporting notifications rather than requiring a mobile-first product from the start.

## Assumptions that need priority validation

These are the most dangerous assumptions:

- One product can serve clubs, organizers, and players without excessive complexity.
- The market needs a replacement rather than an overlay.
- Rankings and stats will drive adoption instead of merely decorating the product.
- Communication can be improved without directly competing with entrenched messaging habits.
